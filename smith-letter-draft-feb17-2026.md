## What's Moving the Market

### Trump Executive Order Targets Institutional Buyers in Housing Market

President Trump signed an executive order on January 20 titled "Stopping Wall Street from Competing with Main Street Homebuyers," directing federal agencies to restrict support for large institutional investor purchases of single-family homes. The order mandates that the Treasury Secretary define "large institutional investor" and "single-family home" by February 19â€”this week. The policy blocks federal programs from facilitating sales to institutional buyers but includes a carve-out for build-to-rent developments. Reuters reports the order directs the administration to "review acquisitions by large investors" and "promote home sales to individual buyers."

Why it matters:

This doesn't ban private transactions, but it cuts off federal funding pipelines. Institutional buyers will still compete where they self-finance, but government-backed deals shift to retail buyers. Watch the Treasury definitionsâ€”if "large" means 100+ properties, small syndicates stay in the game. If it's 10+ properties, the field narrows fast. For operators: this is a positioning moment, not a panic moment.

### Mortgage Affordability Hits Four-Year High as Rates Drop Below 6.1%

Mortgage rate declines in early January pushed housing affordability to a four-year high, according to ICE Mortgage Technology's February 2026 Mortgage Monitor Report. The 30-year fixed-rate mortgage averaged 6.09% as of February 12, down from 6.8% in early 2024. The report shows nearly 5 million borrowers now have refinance opportunities, and the lock-in effect is fading as more homeowners hold mortgages above 6%. NAR economists project affordability will continue improving in 2026 as home prices remain flat (forecast: 2% growth) and household incomes rise faster than monthly payments. Windermere reports housing inventory is now only 9% below February 2020 levels.

Why it matters:

The 2021-2023 lock-in effect is breaking. Inventory is coming back. Motivated sellers who sat out 2023-2024 at 3% rates are now willing to move. Pipeline is filling. Watch for acceleration in Q2-Q3 as buyers realize rates won't drop to 4%. For operators: this is your window. The sellers moving now are the ones who need to, not the ones who want to.

Source: https://www.housingwire.com/articles/mortgage-rates-refinance-2026/

### Housing Reform Bill Passes House 390-9, Legalizes ADUs and Duplexes Nationwide

The U.S. House passed sweeping housing reform legislation on Monday, voting 390-9 to advance the Housing for the 21st Century Act. The bill legalizes duplexes, triplexes, and accessory dwelling units (ADUs) in single-family zones, eliminates parking minimums, streamlines environmental reviews for projects under 15 units, and raises FHA multifamily loan limits by 300-400%. Manufactured housing rules are rewritten to eliminate the permanent chassis requirement, treating factory-built homes on permanent foundations like site-built homes for financing and taxation. HousingWire reports the bill creates pilot grants for cities to develop pre-approved "pattern books"â€”standardized designs that bypass case-by-case reviews.

Why it matters:

This is the most aggressive federal zoning reform in 50 years. Duplex, triplex, and ADU legalization opens conversion plays in restrictive suburban markets. Environmental review exemptions cut 6-12 months off project timelines. Higher FHA limits unlock small multifamily deals that were previously unfeasible. Senate passage expected in Q2â€”start identifying conversion targets now. The market that opens six months from now is being shaped today.

Source: https://www.housingwire.com/articles/what-the-housing-for-the-21st-century-act-means-for-real-estate-agents-lenders/

### Insurance Costs Reshape Homebuyer Decisions as Premiums Rise 24% Since 2021

Home insurance premiums have risen 24% since 2021, and most homeowners expect further increases in 2026, according to a HousingWire report. Nearly half of buyers say insurance costs heavily influence purchasing decisions, with climate risk driving pricing across wildfire, flood, and storm-prone regions. Florida premiums increased an average of $1,450 between 2020-2023, while replacement costs surged 55% between 2020-2022 (Insurance Information Institute data). Severe convective storms, wildfires, and flooding remain primary drivers of underwriting in 2026, with insurers pricing in long-term climate exposure.

Why it matters:

This is a hidden affordability killer. Monthly PITI calculations are breaking in high-risk markets where insurance adds $200-400/month. For operators: underwrite insurance as aggressively as you underwrite property taxes. Coastal and wildfire-adjacent deals that pencil at 6% rates don't pencil at 6% + $350/month insurance. Distressed inventory will build in uninsurable marketsâ€”opportunity for cash buyers who self-insure or know how to structure around it.

### Regional Housing Starts Diverge Sharply as West Contracts, South Expands

U.S. housing starts data shows sharp regional divergence in Q4 2025, with the West down 21.9% to 243,000 units, while the South rose 1.2% to 650,000 units and the Midwest increased 0.5% to 199,000 units, according to Census Bureau data reported by Trading Economics. The Northeast saw a slight decline of 0.6% to 154,000 units. Overall housing starts averaged 118,792 new units per month in 2023 (seasonally adjusted), down 8.11% from 2022. The next housing starts report is scheduled for release February 25.

Why it matters:

National headlines don't tell the whole story. Local fundamentals matter more than macro narratives. The West is contracting (regulation, insurance, costs) while the South absorbs migration and job growth. For operators: watch for supply/demand imbalances in secondary metros. Nashville, Charlotte, Raleigh are building fastâ€”cap rate compression ahead. Boise, Phoenix, Vegas are slowingâ€”distress inventory building. Don't bet on "the housing market."
